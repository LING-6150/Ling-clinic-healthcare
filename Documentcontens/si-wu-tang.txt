Si Wu Tang (Four Substance Decoction) — Classical Blood Nourishing Formula

Si Wu Tang is one of the most fundamental and widely used formulas in Traditional Chinese Medicine, first recorded in the Tang Dynasty medical text Xian Shou Li Shang Xu Duan Mi Fang. It serves as the foundational formula for nourishing and invigorating blood, and forms the basis of dozens of derivative formulas used throughout East Asian medicine.

Composition and Ingredients:
The formula consists of four herbs working in precise combination. Shu Di Huang (Rehmannia glutinosa, prepared) serves as the chief herb, powerfully nourishing Yin and replenishing essence and blood. It addresses the root deficiency of blood and Yin in the body. Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis) acts as the deputy herb, nourishing blood, invigorating blood circulation, and regulating menstruation. It has a moving quality that prevents the formula from becoming too static or cloying. Bai Shao (Paeonia lactiflora, white peony root) nourishes blood, astringes Yin, and softens the liver. It works synergistically with Shu Di Huang to build blood while calming liver Yang. Chuan Xiong (Ligusticum chuanxiong) invigorates blood, promotes the movement of Qi, and alleviates pain. Its ascending and dispersing nature balances the heavy, nourishing quality of the other three herbs.

Treatment Principles:
Si Wu Tang operates on the principle of simultaneously nourishing and moving blood. In TCM theory, blood must both be sufficient in quantity and able to flow freely. A formula that only nourishes without moving risks creating stagnation, while one that only moves without nourishing depletes the blood further. The balanced combination of Shu Di Huang and Bai Shao (nourishing, static) with Dang Gui and Chuan Xiong (nourishing but also moving) achieves this dual action.

Clinical Indications:
Si Wu Tang is indicated for blood deficiency patterns with signs of blood stagnation. Primary indications include irregular menstruation with delayed cycles, scanty or pale menstrual flow, amenorrhea due to blood deficiency, dysmenorrhea with dull cramping pain, postpartum blood deficiency, dizziness and vertigo from blood failing to nourish the head, pale complexion and lips, blurred vision or floaters, palpitations and poor sleep due to heart blood deficiency, numbness or tingling in the extremities from blood not reaching the periphery, and lusterless or brittle nails from insufficient blood nourishment.

Tongue and Pulse Findings:
The characteristic tongue presentation is pale, possibly with a thin white coating. In cases with more stagnation, the tongue may be slightly purple or have purple spots on the edges. The pulse is typically thin and wiry, or thin and choppy, reflecting both blood deficiency and mild stagnation.

Modifications and Derivative Formulas:
Si Wu Tang serves as the base for numerous important clinical formulas. Ba Zhen Tang (Eight Treasure Decoction) combines Si Wu Tang with Si Jun Zi Tang to address both Qi and blood deficiency simultaneously, used when fatigue and weakness accompany blood deficiency symptoms. Tao Hong Si Wu Tang adds Tao Ren (peach kernel) and Hong Hua (safflower) to significantly strengthen blood-invigorating action, used for more pronounced blood stagnation with darker menstrual blood and clots. Sheng Hua Tang modifies the formula specifically for postpartum recovery, promoting the expulsion of retained lochia while nourishing new blood. Jiao Ai Tang adds Ai Ye (mugwort) and E Jiao (donkey hide gelatin) to warm the uterus and stop bleeding, used for cold-type uterine bleeding during pregnancy.

Dosage and Preparation:
Traditional dosage uses Shu Di Huang 12g, Dang Gui 10g, Bai Shao 10g, and Chuan Xiong 6g, though modern practitioners adjust based on individual presentations. The formula is traditionally prepared as a decoction by simmering in water for 30-45 minutes. Modern preparations include concentrated granules and patent pill forms.

Contraindications and Cautions:
Si Wu Tang should be used cautiously in cases of spleen deficiency with dampness, as the cloying nature of Shu Di Huang may impair digestion. It is contraindicated in cases of excessive bleeding without deficiency, and should be modified or avoided during acute illness with exterior pathogens. Pregnant women should use it only under professional guidance, particularly formulas containing Chuan Xiong which has blood-moving properties.

Modern Research:
Contemporary pharmacological research has investigated the individual components and combined formula. Studies suggest the formula may support hematopoiesis by promoting red blood cell production, improve microcirculation, regulate hormonal balance particularly estrogen and progesterone levels, demonstrate anti-inflammatory effects, and provide antioxidant protection. Clinical trials have explored its application in treating iron deficiency anemia, gynecological disorders, and post-chemotherapy blood deficiency in cancer patients.
