Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis) is one of the most important herbs in Traditional Chinese Medicine. It is commonly used to nourish and invigorate blood, regulate menstruation, and calm the mind. Modern research suggests it contains ferulic acid and polysaccharides that may have anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory effects. It is often prescribed for insomnia caused by blood deficiency, fatigue, pale complexion, and irregular menstruation.

Huang Qi (Astragalus membranaceus) is a foundational tonic herb in TCM used to tonify Wei Qi, the defensive energy that protects the body from external pathogens. It strengthens the immune system, reduces fatigue, and improves physical stamina. Modern studies show it contains astragalosides that stimulate T-cell and NK-cell activity. It is commonly prescribed for chronic fatigue, spontaneous sweating, frequent colds, and poor appetite.

Suan Zao Ren (Ziziphus jujuba seed) is the primary herb in TCM for treating insomnia and anxiety. It nourishes the heart and liver, calms the mind, and anchors the spirit. It is most effective for insomnia caused by heart and liver blood deficiency, characterized by difficulty falling asleep, palpitations, night sweats, and irritability. Modern pharmacological studies show it contains jujubosides that have sedative and anxiolytic effects.

Bai Zi Ren (Biota orientalis seed) nourishes the heart, calms the mind, and moistens the intestines. It is used for insomnia, forgetfulness, palpitations, and constipation in the elderly. It works synergistically with Suan Zao Ren for heart blood deficiency patterns.

He Huan Hua (Albizzia julibrissin flower) is known as the happiness flower in TCM. It relieves constraint of liver Qi, calms the mind, and alleviates depression and anxiety. It is particularly effective for insomnia caused by emotional disturbance, stress, and irritability.

Ren Shen (Panax ginseng) is the most famous tonic herb in TCM. It powerfully tonifies Yuan Qi, the source energy of the body. It is used for extreme fatigue, shortness of breath, poor appetite, and impaired cognitive function. Modern research confirms its adaptogenic properties, helping the body resist physical and mental stress. It contains ginsenosides that enhance energy metabolism and improve concentration.

Gou Qi Zi (Lycium barbarum) nourishes liver and kidney Yin, brightens the eyes, and moistens the lungs. It is used for blurred vision, dizziness, lower back pain, and premature aging. Modern studies show it is rich in zeaxanthin, betaine, and polysaccharides that have antioxidant and neuroprotective effects.

Ban Xia (Pinellia ternata) dries dampness, transforms phlegm, descends rebellious Qi, and stops vomiting. It is the primary herb for treating nausea, vomiting, and phlegm accumulation. It is commonly used for digestive issues, morning sickness, and phlegm-type insomnia where the mind is disturbed by phlegm heat.

Chai Hu (Bupleurum chinense) spreads liver Qi, resolves Shaoyang disorders, and lifts Yang Qi. It is essential for treating liver Qi stagnation patterns characterized by chest and flank distension, emotional depression, irritability, and alternating chills and fever.

Fu Ling (Poria cocos) calms the mind, strengthens the spleen, and drains dampness. It is used for insomnia, palpitations, poor memory, dizziness, and digestive weakness. It is one of the most widely used herbs in TCM formulas due to its gentle tonifying and calming properties.
